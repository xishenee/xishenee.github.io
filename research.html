Ultra-fast Optical Correlators

Opto-electronic PMT Pre-processor    In order to achieve shift, scale, and rotation invariant (SSRI) correlation, it is necessary to pre-process the input images to convert any rotation and scaling into linear shifts. The polar Mellin transform (PMT) is an especially good candidate for this pre-processing as it can be calculated as the log-polar transform (LPT) of the magnitude of the FT of an image. Because of this, optics can be used for the FT part of the operation, while an electronic module can be used for the log-polar tranform (LPT). The LPT can be computed parallelly either by FPGA or a multi-core CPU.


OPP

Hybrid Opto-electronic Joint Transform Correlator    The hybrid opto-electronic joint transform correlator (HOEJTC) combines the OPP stage with a joint transform correlator to create an SSRI image correlator, enabling target recognition at speeds of up to 720 fps with a response time of just 1.4 milliseconds. By replacing the fragile nonlinear materials in traditional JTCs with electronics, the HOEJTC enhances the system stability but keeps the speed.


HOJTC

Balanced Joint Transform Correlator    The balanced JTC eliminates the two independent intensity terms in the joint power spectrum of a traditional JTC, which otherwise dominate the final correlation signal. We plan to upgrade it to achieve a full-speed 720 fps.


BJTC

Spatio-temporal Holographic Correlator

Spatio-temporal Holographic Correlator    In the Spatio-temporal holographic correlator (STHC), the atoms are inhomogeneously broadened (IHB’d) such that the spread of resonant frequencies covers the desired temporal frequency bandwidth for the system. The temporal information resulting from the interference between a plain pulse with a wide spectrum and the spectral components of the reference video are stored in the atoms in the form of coherence of different atomic states, forming a grating in the frequency domain. The spectral components of the query video diffract off these gratings to reproduce the plain pulse in case of a match, akin to the process employed for stimulated photon echoes. Groups of these atoms can be placed in a 2D array at the Fourier plane of a lens such that each group forms a pixel that covers a small range of the spatial frequencies, thus enabling simultaneous 2D spatial and 1D temporal correlation.


STHC

Numerical and Analytical Simulation    The evolution of the atomic system can be numerically calculated by solving the Schrödinger equation. Simulation results demonstrate that this system can function as a spatio-temporal correlator for automatic event recognition. The analytica model is developed as a three dimensional transfer function of the system, and shown that implementing the transfer function yields the same result as the numerical atomic model, but at a faster speed.


STHC Simulation

Speed Invariant Event Correlator    To detect events with differet playback speeds in a video database, a two-step method (TSM) is developed to recover the time-shift parameter while still achieving speed-invariant spatio-temporal correlation. In Step I, we carry out MT of the time-domain signal for each pixel, also for both videos. The MT’d videos are input into a STHC at a speed determined by the storage medium, producing the scale factor ln(α) between the query video and the event in the database. In Step II, the query clip is adjusted by the factor obtained from Step I to align its playback speed with the events in the reference clip. These aligned videos are then input into the STHC again. The resulting correlation signal produces the temporal position of the event.


Speed invariant STHC

Practical Implementation    For practical implementation, an off-resonant three-level system is used to create an effective two-level system, ensuring a sufficiently long memory time. One approach to leveraging the short loading time is to use a high-speed SLM. Additionally, holographic techniques can be explored to maximize the benefits of the atomic medium’s fast loading time. A magneto-optical trap (MOT) is constructed to cool and trap the atoms.

COMSOL Simulation

Arbitary Shaped Volum Holograms    Gratings formed from converging or diverging beams present curved profiles that vary with the position inside the material. One approach to analyze these types of holographic gratings is to use a finite element method (FEM) to search for a steady-state solution for the wave equation of a beam propagating through, and diffracting from, the grating. COMSOL is used to simulate the diffraction of holographic gratings with non-trivial profiles.

Ring Resonator    To gain a better understanding of the optical dynamics with the ring resonator, the finite element method in COMSOL Multiphysics is used to simulation the optical field present in this ring resonator. The simulated free spectral range aligns well with theoretical calculations.
